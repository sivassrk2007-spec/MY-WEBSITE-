Brand Summary Template

The 5-Sentence Formula

Not a mission statement. A diagnostic. Plain language that describes what the business is, who it serves, what the gap is, and what the opportunity looks like.

"The gap between what a brand delivers and what people believe it delivers — that is the entire branding problem."

Layer 1 Principle
Sentence 1 — What It Is
[Brand] is a [specific type] in [geography] that serves [specific audience].
Sentence 2 — What It Does Well
It has [real capability / credential] that most competitors in this space do not — [specific proof point].
Sentence 3 — Pricing Reality
Positioned at [price tier] — above [lower competitor] and below [premium competitor] — creating a specific value narrative that must be communicated clearly.
Sentence 4 — The Gap
Despite this, the brand currently [appears / is perceived] like [the problem] — meaning a qualified buyer cannot distinguish it from [the competition].
Sentence 5 — The Opportunity
The core branding opportunity is to [specific repositioning action] and own the position no competitor currently holds: [ownable position].
Layer 02

Brand Strategy Engine

Strategy is not a tagline. It is the decision framework that governs every word, image, and experience the brand produces. Build it once. Apply it everywhere.

01
ELEMENT 01
Brand Positioning Statement
One sentence that answers: who is this for, what does it offer, and why is it the only logical choice? It is not a tagline — it is the internal compass that governs every external decision.
Formula: [Brand] is for [specific audience] who want [outcome] — unlike [competitor] who [limitation], we [differentiator].
02
ELEMENT 02
Core Brand Promise
What the brand commits to — every time, at every touchpoint, without exception. Not an aspiration. A commitment that, if broken, damages trust. The promise must be specific enough to be testable.
Rule: If you cannot evaluate whether your brand kept or broke this promise on any given day, it is not specific enough. Rewrite it.
ELEMENT 03
Brand Archetype
The archetype is the personality framework that makes the brand feel like a person — consistent, recognisable, trustworthy. Choose one primary and one secondary. Every competitor who picks the same archetype is a warning: you need to go deeper into what makes this brand's version of the archetype genuinely different.
The Caregiver
Protective. Empathetic. Reliable. Puts others first.
Best for: Healthcare, Education, NGOs, Family brands
The Sage
Expert. Thoughtful. Credible. Seeks truth.
Best for: Consulting, Technology, Research, Academia
The Ruler
Authoritative. Premium. In control. Sets standards.
Best for: Luxury, Finance, Government, Enterprise
The Hero
Bold. Capable. Transformative. Overcomes challenges.

04
ELEMENT 04
Differentiation Strategy
The single most important strategic decision. What makes this brand the only logical choice for its specific audience — not a better choice, but the only one. Generic differentiators (quality, service, value) are not differentiators. They are table stakes.
Test: If a competitor could say the same thing without lying, it is not a differentiator. Find what only this brand can own — and own it loudly.
ELEMENT 05
Brand Personality
3–5 traits that define how the brand speaks, acts, and shows up — consistently, across every channel and every team member. Traits must be specific enough to exclude their opposites. "Professional" excludes nothing. "Direct without being cold" excludes a lot.
Calm under pressureSpecific over vagueEmpathetic not sentimentalAuthoritative not coldAccessible not casual
ELEMENT 06
Messaging Pillars
3–5 themes that anchor all brand communication. Pillars are not topics — they are the recurring beliefs the brand builds over time. Every piece of content should be traceable back to one pillar. If it cannot be, it should not be published.
P1
What We Stand For
The core belief that drives every decision. Not what the brand sells — what it believes about the world and the people it serves. This pillar earns trust before a product is ever considered.
P2
What We Know
The expertise and depth that no generalist competitor can match. This pillar demonstrates credibility through specificity — data, insights, outcomes, named expertise.
P3
Who We Serve
The audience, named and described with precision. This pillar makes the audience feel seen — not marketed to. When people see themselves in a brand's communication, they stop scrolling.
P4
What We Deliver
Specific outcomes, not vague promises. Patient recovery rates. Client ROI. Project delivery timelines. This pillar builds proof — converting belief into decision.
P5
What Sets Us Apart
The differentiation pillar. Where competitors overlap, this brand diverges. Named, specific, defensible. This pillar is the reason a buyer chooses this brand when all other variables appear equal.

Every page on the website has one job. If it cannot be stated in one sentence, the page needs to be rewritten. These are the three pages every brand must get right before anything else.

Section 01 — Hero / Homepage

The 10-Second Test

A stranger must understand — within 10 seconds — exactly what this brand does, who it is for, and why it is different. If they cannot, the hero section has failed, regardless of how it looks.

HeadlineOne sentence. Names the audience, outcome, or differentiator. Not a slogan. A statement of fact.
Subtext2–3 sentences that expand the headline. What the brand does, for whom, and why it matters. Specific nouns. No abstract claims.
Primary CTAAction-first. "Book a Consultation" not "Learn More." Names the next step, not a vague direction.
Trust SignalOne credibility anchor above the fold: accreditation, years of operation, client count, or a specific proof point. Not a logo wall — one specific fact.
Section 02 — About

Why This Brand Exists

The About section is not a company history. It is the brand's belief statement — why it was built, what it stands against, and what it is committed to. Told as a story, not a timeline.

OpeningThe problem that existed before this brand. What the market was getting wrong. Written from the audience's perspective, not the founder's ego.
The BeliefWhat this brand believes should be true for its audience. The founding conviction. One sentence, sharp and specific.
The ProofHow long, how many, what results. Track record in plain language — numbers that mean something to the reader, not the brand.
The PeopleNamed faces. Not "our team of experts" — specific people with specific roles and specific credentials. Humans build trust. Anonymity destroys it.
Section 03 — Services / Offerings

Clarity Over Cleverness

Each service page answers one question: what problem does this solve and what happens next? Jargon is the enemy of conversion. A buyer who does not understand an offering will not buy it.

Problem FirstLead with the condition, challenge, or need — in the words the audience uses to describe it. Not the solution name.
What HappensThe process in 3–5 steps. Specific enough to reduce fear, simple enough to be read in 30 seconds.
The OutcomeWhat the audience walks away with. Specific result, not a promise. "Average recovery: 6 days." Not "faster recovery."
Next StepOne action. Book, Call, Download, Apply. Not a contact form buried at the bottom of the page.

Social media content that does not connect to a messaging pillar is noise. These five content themes ensure every post builds the brand — not just fills the calendar.

THEME 01
The Expert Voice
Specialist-authored content that answers one specific question your audience is already asking. Not brand promotion — genuine knowledge transfer. Published under a named person's authority, not the brand account alone.
Why it works: People follow people before they follow brands. Expertise builds trust before a sale is ever contemplated.
THEME 02
The Real Story
Client or customer journeys — specific, named (with permission), and outcome-focused. Not a testimonial. A narrative: the problem they had, what happened, and what changed. Details make it believable. Vagueness makes it forgettable.
Why it works: Proof is more persuasive than any claim. A specific story is more credible than the best-written case study.
THEME 03
The Informed Audience
Educational content that makes the audience smarter about their own situation — before they need to buy. How to evaluate a competitor. What questions to ask before committing. What the industry gets wrong. This content earns loyalty before the conversation begins.
Why it works: Brands that educate before they sell are trusted. Brands that only sell are tuned out.
THEME 04
Behind the Brand
The people, the process, and the principles that make this brand different. Not polished PR — authentic glimpses of how the work is actually done. Staff spotlights. Process walkthroughs. Decision-making moments. The human texture of the organisation.
Why it works: Transparency builds familiarity. Familiarity builds trust. Trust converts.
THEME 05
The Strong Opinion
A clear, argued point of view on something the industry debates, avoids, or gets wrong. Not controversy for attention — genuine conviction backed by reasoning. Brands that stand for something attract the right audience and repel the wrong one. Both outcomes are valuable.
Why it works: Neutrality is forgettable. A brand with a point of view earns followers who actively agree — and that is the foundation of community.
THEME 06
The Proof Post
Numbers, milestones, certifications, and results — communicated in language that means something to the audience, not the brand. "1,200 patients served this quarter" means more than "we're proud of our growth." Specificity converts browsers into believers.
Why it works: In markets with high trust deficits, proof is the only currency that matters. Every number is a reason to believe.

An ad has one job: interrupt the current behaviour of the right person and give them a reason to stop. Hook lines are the entire battle. If the first line fails, everything else is unread.

01
Proximity Hook
"You don't need to drive to the city for specialist care."
Targets geography-based convenience. Works when the brand has a location advantage the audience hasn't considered. Names the friction point before naming the solution.
02
Trust Contrast Hook
"The difference between a listed hospital and one that follows up."
Positions against category norms without naming competitors. Creates immediate curiosity and implies the brand is on the better side of the distinction.
03
Fear-Relief Hook
"Most people wait too long. Here's what early looks like."
Addresses the audience's most common mistake. Positions the brand as the authority that prevents regret. Earns attention through relevance, not alarm.
04
Credential Hook
"NABH accredited. The same standard. A fraction of the wait."
Leads with a verifiable trust signal and contrasts it against a known pain point. Combines credibility with convenience — a high-conversion combination.
05
Specificity Hook
"Dr. Karthik has performed 200+ robotic surgeries. He sees patients on Tuesdays."
Named expert + specific number + accessible scheduling. Three trust signals in one sentence. Specificity is the enemy of scepticism.
06
Heritage Hook
"The institution that educated your family. Now taking care of their health."
For brands with institutional legacy, this hook transfers existing trust from a known entity to a new service. Avoids building credibility from zero.

Tone of voice is the personality of the brand in language. It must be specific enough to create consistency and flexible enough to work across contexts. These are not rules — they are guardrails.

VOICE TRAIT 01

Specific

Name things precisely. "14 years of cardiac surgery" not "decades of experience." Numbers, names, and outcomes — not adjectives.

VOICE TRAIT 02

Direct

Say what needs to be said in the fewest words. No filler phrases. No hedging. "We follow up after every procedure" — not "we are committed to ongoing patient engagement."

VOICE TRAIT 03

Human

Write the way a knowledgeable, caring person speaks — not a corporate communications team. Read every sentence out loud. If it sounds unnatural when spoken, rewrite it.

Say This
"Dr. Ramesh has treated 400+ cardiac patients. He is here every Tuesday."
"We call every patient on Day 3 after discharge. Every time."
"NABH accreditation means your surgery room meets global hygiene standards. Not a badge — a daily protocol."
"Your family can reach us at any hour. Not a call centre — the ward nurse on duty."
"We explained everything before the procedure. We'll explain everything after."
Never Say This
"Our highly qualified team of experts is dedicated to your wellbeing."
"We are committed to delivering world-class healthcare solutions."
"State-of-the-art technology combined with compassionate care."
"Patient satisfaction is at the core of everything we do."
"We pride ourselves on our comprehensive approach to health and wellness."

The brand promise breaks or holds at the human level. How leadership speaks sets the culture. How staff speak to patients, clients, and partners is the brand in action — not a poster on the wall.

Leadership Communication
Speak in outcomes, not slogansIn every internal meeting, reference patients, clients, or customers by name or specific situation — not as abstractions. "The family in Ward 4 needs an update" not "we must prioritise patient satisfaction."
Name the standard, don't assume itWhen the brand commits to follow-up calls, define the exact protocol: who makes the call, by when, what they say, how it is logged. Aspirations without systems produce inconsistency.
Celebrate the brand promise keptShare examples — in team meetings, in internal communications — of moments where a team member exemplified the brand. Culture is built through the stories leadership chooses to tell repeatedly.
Acknowledge when the promise was brokenIf a patient was left without information, if a delivery was missed, if a follow-up didn't happen — name it, understand why, and build the system that prevents recurrence. Brands that ignore internal failures build external credibility on unstable ground.
Staff-to-Customer Communication
Address by name from first contactEvery customer, client, or patient is greeted and referred to by name from the moment they register — not by room number, case type, or account number. This single habit changes the entire experience.
Never leave a question unansweredIf a staff member does not know the answer: "I don't know right now, but I will find out and come back to you within 15 minutes." Then follow through. Silence is the fastest trust destroyer in high-stakes environments.
Close every interaction with a next stepEvery conversation ends with clarity: what happens next, when it happens, and who is responsible. The customer should never have to ask "what now?" If they do, the interaction is incomplete.
Match external brand languageStaff who speak formally when the brand is warm, or casually when the brand is authoritative, create friction. Brand voice training is not optional — it is the brand's quality control system.

Video Script Framework
YouTube, Instagram Reels, LinkedIn video, website explainer. One structure — adapted for duration and platform.

The 90-Second Authority Video

For LinkedIn, YouTube, and website homepage. Builds credibility through a named expert addressing one specific problem with clarity and conviction.

0:00–0:08
The Hook
One sentence that names the problem the audience already has. On-camera. No intro, no logo, no music. The first word earns the next 82 seconds.
0:08–0:20
Why It Matters
The real consequence of ignoring this problem. Specific — not "it could be serious" but "most people who experience this wait an average of 14 months before seeking help."
0:20–0:55
The Expert Insight
The one thing worth knowing. The specialist's point of view — direct, clear, based on clinical experience. No jargon. References a real pattern they see regularly.
0:55–1:15
The Proof
A brief case reference (anonymised): "We had a patient last month who..." — specific detail, specific outcome. Makes the insight concrete and credible.
1:15–1:30
The One Action
One specific next step. "If you've had this symptom for more than two weeks, book a consultation this week. Not next month." Specific, urgent, actionable.

The 30-Second Reel / Short

For Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts. One idea. Zero padding. Maximum impact in minimum time.

0:00–0:03
Hook Frame
The most surprising or counterintuitive statement. On screen as text AND spoken. "The most common heart attack symptom isn't chest pain."

The One Truth
Deliver the single insight. No preamble. Direct. The audience should feel they've learned something in 12 seconds.
0:15–0:25
The Why It Matters
One sentence on the consequence of not knowing this. Makes the insight stick.
0:25–0:30
The Save Prompt
"Save this. You'll want to share it." Not a sales CTA — a shareability prompt. These posts spread when they're saved and sent.

CONTENT RULE 01

Pillar Before Platform

Decide which messaging pillar a piece belongs to before deciding which platform it goes on. Platform is distribution. Pillar is purpose.

CONTENT RULE 02

Repurpose Vertically

One doctor interview becomes a LinkedIn post, an Instagram carousel, a 60-second Reel, and a blog article. One source of truth. Four distribution formats.

CONTENT RULE 03

Measure What Matters

Reach is vanity. Saves, shares, DMs, and consultation bookings are signal. Track the metric that maps to the business goal — not the one that feels good in a report.

Layer 05

Brand Audit Framework

A brand audit is not a creative review. It is a diagnostic. The question is not "does this look good?" — it is "does this do what it needs to do for the business?" Four dimensions. Evaluated without sentiment.

"Do not soften the audit. Brands improve when problems are named clearly, not when they are managed diplomatically."

Layer 5 Principle

What to Audit

Website — Homepage, About, Service pages, Contact
Social Media — Last 30 posts across all active platforms
Ad Creative — Last 3 active campaigns or last 6 months
Physical — Signage, brochures, letterheads, office space
Human — Front desk, call handling, email language
Third-party — Google profile, Practo, JustDial, reviews
🔍
Clarity
"Does a stranger understand what this brand does in 10 seconds?"
Test: Show homepage to someone unfamiliar for 10 seconds. Ask: what does this company do? Who is it for? If they cannot answer — clarity has failed.
🔗
Consistency
"Does it sound and feel like the same brand everywhere?"
Test: Read a tweet, an ad, a brochure, and a staff email back-to-back. Do they share a voice? If they sound like four different companies, consistency has failed.
⬡
Differentiation
"Is there a clear reason to choose this brand over competitors?"
Test: Replace the brand name with a competitor's name. If the content still works — there is no differentiation. The brand must say something only it can say.
🏛
Credibility
"Do trust signals exist — and do they land?"
Test: List every credential, accreditation, and proof point on the website. Is each one explained in terms of what it means for the audience? If not — credibility is being wasted.

What Is Not Working. Why. What to Do.

High Priority
FIX 01
The hero section fails the 10-second test
What's wrong
A first-time visitor cannot identify the brand's specialty, audience, or differentiator within 10 seconds of landing on the homepage. The headline could belong to any hospital in the country.
Exact fix
Rewrite the hero headline to name the top 2 clinical capabilities, the geography, and one trust signal. Example: "Robotic Surgery. NABH-Accredited Cancer Care. Right here in Kattankulathur." Test with 5 people unfamiliar with the brand before publishing.
High Priority
FIX 02
Doctor profiles build no trust
What's wrong
Current profiles show name and degree only. In healthcare, the doctor IS the product. A patient deciding between hospitals wants to know: who will treat me, what have they done, and can I trust them? None of these questions are answered.
Exact fix
Rebuild every doctor profile with: professional photograph, 2-sentence plain-English biography, years of experience, number of procedures performed, and one direct quote from the doctor about their philosophy of care. Add "Book with [Name]" CTA to each profile.
High Priority
FIX 03
Brand voice has no consistency across channels
What's wrong
The website, Instagram, Facebook, and printed materials sound like they were produced by four different teams with no shared brief. When a brand sounds different everywhere, audiences assume it is unreliable everywhere.
Exact fix
Create a single one-page Brand Voice Document: 3 words the brand sounds like, 3 it never sounds like, 2 example sentences for each channel, and one real before/after example. Distribute to every person who creates content. No exception.
Medium Priority
FIX 04
NABH / NABL credentials are invisible trust signals
What's wrong
Both accreditations appear in the footer and in a features list — alongside "free Wi-Fi" and "24/7 ambulance." This gives them equal weight to amenities, completely wasting their trust value.
Exact fix
Create a dedicated "Why Our Accreditation Matters" section. For NABH: "This means your operating room meets the same hygiene standard as any internationally accredited hospital. Not a badge — an audited protocol we maintain every day." Make it visible on every service page, not just the homepage.
Medium Priority
FIX 05
No post-treatment brand presence
What's wrong
The brand relationship ends at billing. There is no follow-up communication, no discharge guidance content, no review request, no ongoing connection. This is the single most expensive missed opportunity in building patient loyalty and referral.
Exact fix
Build a 3-touch post-discharge sequence: Day 3 follow-up call (check on recovery), Day 14 reminder for review appointment, Month 1 relevant health tip from the treating specialist. This costs almost nothing. It generates testimonials, referrals, and return visits.
Quick Win
FIX 06
Google Business Profile is under-optimised
What's wrong
For most patients, the Google Business Profile is the first brand impression — before the website. Incomplete profiles, unresponded reviews, and outdated information undermine the brand before a visit is considered.
Exact fix
Complete every field. Add 20+ real facility photos. Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Add the correct speciality categories. Post one Google update per week. This single step improves both trust and local search ranking.
